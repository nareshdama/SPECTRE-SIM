\begin{abstract}
Modern active radar and infrared missile guidance systems rely on
the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) to estimate the line-of-sight
(LOS) rate from seeker measurements; this estimate directly drives
the proportional navigation (PN) guidance law to generate intercept
commands. While false data injection (FDI) attacks against
Kalman filter–based control systems have been extensively studied
in cyber-physical and unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) contexts, no
open-literature work has examined the EKF seeker loop of a
PN-guided missile as an adversarial attack surface. This paper
addresses that gap by formulating and validating, entirely through
simulation, a kinematically consistent false target injection
framework targeting the EKF measurement channel of a PN-guided
missile seeker. We analytically derive the critical injection rate
boundary below which the false measurement remains within the
chi-squared innovation gate, rendering the attack statistically
covert against standard bad-data detectors. Through a
two-dimensional engagement geometry implemented in Python, we
demonstrate that (i) the resulting miss distance scales
proportionally with injection rate, the scaling governed by a
stabilized proportionality coefficient \(C_a\); (ii) the miss
vector direction is controllable by varying the injection angle
in the engagement plane; and (iii) the attack exploits the EKF's
own post-acquisition covariance convergence to achieve
repeatability across trials. These findings establish a previously
undocumented vulnerability in missile terminal homing guidance and
provide a quantitative framework for evaluating countermeasure
robustness in adversarial electronic warfare (EW) environments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Problem Statement}
\label{sec:intro}
% ================================================================

\subsection{Motivation}

Proportional navigation (PN) is the dominant guidance law for
terminal homing missiles and is implemented in virtually all modern
surface-to-air, air-to-air, and anti-ship missile
systems~\cite{Zarchan2012}. In modern strapdown seekers, the raw
angular measurements are processed by an Extended Kalman Filter
(EKF) to estimate the line-of-sight (LOS) rate
\(\dot{\lambda}\), which drives the PN guidance command as:

\begin{equation}
    a_c = N \cdot V_c \cdot \dot{\hat{\lambda}}
    \label{eq:PN}
\end{equation}

\noindent Where \(a_c\) is the lateral acceleration command,
\(N\) is the PN navigation constant, \(V_c\) is the closing
velocity, and \(\dot{\hat{\lambda}}\) is the EKF-estimated LOS
rate.

Filter measurement integrity is maintained through a chi-squared
innovation monitor, which rejects measurements whose innovation
residual \(\bm{r}_k\) exceeds the normalized threshold:

\begin{equation}
    \bm{r}_k^T \bm{S}_k^{-1} \bm{r}_k \leq \chi^2_{\alpha, n_z}
    \label{eq:chi2}
\end{equation}

\noindent where \(\bm{S}_k\) is the innovation covariance, and
\(\chi^2_{\alpha, n_z}\) is the chi-squared threshold at
the confidence level \(\alpha\) and \(n_z\) measurement dimensions

\subsection{Problem Statement}

\begin{definition}[Kinematically Consistent False Target]
    A \textit{kinematically consistent false target} is a
    synthetic radar or infrared return whose position, velocity,
    and acceleration characteristics are physically realizable by
    a maneuvering object, such that it does not violate the EKF
    dynamic model and therefore generates bounded innovation
    residuals.
\end{definition}

We formally state the research problem as follows:

\vspace{0.5em}
\begin{center}
\fbox{%
    \parbox{0.9\columnwidth}{%
        \textbf{Research Problem:} In a proportional navigation
        guidance system where a missile tracks a target using an
        EKF to estimate line-of-sight rate from seeker
        measurements, determine whether a kinematically consistent
        false target return—injected into the seeker measurement
        channel at a controlled ramp rate—can systematically bias
        the EKF state estimate sufficiently to produce a
        predictable, directionally controlled miss distance while
        remaining statistically undetectable by the standard
        chi-squared innovation monitor.
    }%
}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.5em}

The problem decomposes into three sub-questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Effectiveness:} Does ramp-rate injection produce
    a proportional, deterministic miss distance governed by an
    injection coefficient \(C_a\)?
    \item \textbf{Stealthiness:} Does a critical injection rate
    \(\dot{I}^*\) exist below which the attack bypasses the
    chi-squared gate~\eqref{eq:chi2}?
    \item \textbf{Controllability:} Is the miss vector direction
    independently controllable by varying the injection direction
    \(\theta_\mathrm{inj}\) in the engagement plane?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Gap}

While FDI attacks on Kalman filter–based systems have been
extensively characterized in power grids~\cite{Liu2011},
cyber-physical systems~\cite{Pasqualetti2013}, and UAV
guidance~\cite{Jares2023}, no open-literature study has:
\begin{itemize}
    \item modeled the missile EKF seeker loop as an adversarial
    attack surface in the context of PN terminal homing;
    \item derived the minimum stealthy injection rate for seeker
    measurement channels; or
    \item demonstrated directional control of the miss vector
    through injection angle manipulation.
\end{itemize}

This paper addresses all three gaps through a fully
simulation-based study that requires no classified system
parameters.

\section{Research Hypotheses}
\label{sec:hypotheses}
% ================================================================

Three falsifiable hypotheses are stated for validation by
simulation:

\begin{hypothesis}[Proportional Miss Distance and Directional
Control --- H1]
    A ramp-rate false target injection applied to the EKF
    measurement channel of a PN-guided missile seeker will produce
    a miss distance \(D_m\) proportional to the injection rate
    \(\dot{I}\):
    \begin{equation}
        D_m = C_a \cdot \dot{I} + \varepsilon
        \label{eq:H1}
    \end{equation}
    where \(C_a\) is the miss distance proportionality coefficient
    and \(\varepsilon\) is zero-mean stochastic noise. Furthermore,
    the direction of \(\bm{D}_m\) in the two-dimensional engagement
    plane is controllable by varying the injection angle
    \(\theta_\mathrm{inj}\).
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}[Covert Injection Threshold --- H2]
    There exists a critical injection rate \(\dot{I}^*\) below
    which the injected false measurement satisfies the EKF
    chi-squared innovation gate:
    \begin{equation}
        \dot{I} \leq \dot{I}^* \implies
        \bm{r}_k^T \bm{S}_k^{-1} \bm{r}_k \leq
        \chi^2_{\alpha, n_z}
        \quad \forall\, k
        \label{eq:H2}
    \end{equation}
    and that \(\dot{I}^*\) is analytically derivable from the
    steady-state EKF measurement noise covariance \(\bm{R}\) and
    the innovation covariance \(\bm{S}_k\).
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}[Covariance Convergence and Attack
Repeatability—H3]
    After seeker acquisition lock, the EKF Kalman gain
    \(\bm{K}_k\) converges to a steady-state value
    \(\bm{K}_\infty\), stabilizing the proportionality coefficient
    \(C_a\) in~\eqref{eq:H1} to a fixed scalar. This convergence
    is exploitable: an attacker with access only to side-channel
    timing information (acquisition lock signal) can predict
    \(C_a\) and therefore achieve precise directional control of
    the miss vector without knowledge of the missile's internal
    state-space parameters.
\end{hypothesis}

\section{Simulation Design and Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ================================================================

\subsection{Engagement Geometry}

The two-dimensional engagement model propagates missile and target
states using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta (RK4) integrator with
timestep \(\Delta t = 0.01\)~s. The missile begins at the origin
with initial velocity \(v_{m,x} = 300\)~m/s,
\(v_{m,y} = 0\)~m/s. The target is initialized at
\((x_T, y_T) = (10{,}000, 500)\)~m with velocity
\(v_{T,x} = -200\)~m/s, \(v_{T,y} = 0\)~m/s, producing a
closing geometry with initial range \(r_0 \approx 10{,}012\)~m
and closing velocity \(V_c \approx 500\)~m/s. Intercept is
declared when the missile-to-target range falls below
\(r_{\min} = 1\)~m, or the simulation terminates at
\(t_{\max} = 60\)~s.

\subsection{EKF Seeker Model}

The seeker is modeled as a bearing-only Extended Kalman Filter
(EKF) with state vector
\(\mathbf{x} = [r_x,\; r_y,\; \dot{r}_x,\; \dot{r}_y]^T\),
representing relative position and velocity between target and
missile. The nonlinear measurement function is:
%
\begin{equation}
  h(\mathbf{x}) = \arctan_2(r_y,\; r_x)
  \label{eq:measurement}
\end{equation}
%
with measurement Jacobian:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{H} = \left[
    \frac{-r_y}{r^2+\epsilon},\;
    \frac{r_x}{r^2+\epsilon},\;
    0,\; 0
  \right]
  \label{eq:jacobian}
\end{equation}
%
where \(r^2 = r_x^2 + r_y^2\) and \(\epsilon = 10^{-9}\)
prevents division by zero. The constant-velocity state
transition matrix is:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F} = \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 0 & \Delta t & 0 \\
    0 & 1 & 0 & \Delta t \\
    0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & 0 & 1
  \end{bmatrix}
  \label{eq:transition}
\end{equation}
%
Process noise covariance is
\(\mathbf{Q} = \mathrm{diag}(0.1,\, 0.1,\, 1.0,\, 1.0)\)
and scalar measurement noise variance is
\(R = 1 \times 10^{-4}\)~rad\(^2\). Covariance updates use
the Joseph stabilized form to ensure positive semi-definiteness
throughout the engagement. EKF acquisition lock is declared
when \(\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{P}) < 50.0\) after a minimum
acquisition time \(t_{\mathrm{acq}} = 2.0\)~s.

\subsection{Proportional Navigation Guidance Law}

The lateral acceleration command follows the classical
Proportional Navigation (PN) law~\cite{Zarchan2012}:
%
\begin{equation}
  a_c = N \cdot V_c \cdot \hat{\dot{\lambda}}
  \label{eq:PN_law}
\end{equation}
%
where \(N = 4\) is the navigation constant,
\(V_c\) is the closing velocity, and
\(\hat{\dot{\lambda}}\) is the LOS rate estimated by the EKF.
Commands are saturated at
\(a_{\max} = 300\)~m/s\(^2\) to model physical actuator
limits. The LOS rate estimate is extracted from the EKF
posterior state as:
%
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\dot{\lambda}} =
  \frac{\hat{r}_x \hat{\dot{r}}_y
        - \hat{r}_y \hat{\dot{r}}_x}
       {\hat{r}_x^2 + \hat{r}_y^2 + \epsilon}
  \label{eq:los_rate}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Adversarial Injection Attack Model}

The attacker injects a kinematically consistent ramp offset
into the EKF angular bearing measurement channel after
acquisition lock. The injected measurement is:
%
\begin{equation}
  z_{\mathrm{inj}}(t) = z_{\mathrm{true}}(t)
    + \dot{I}\,(t - t_{\mathrm{lock}})
      \cos(\theta_{\mathrm{inj}})
  \label{eq:attack}
\end{equation}
%
where \(\dot{I}\) is the injection rate [rad/s per second],
\(t_{\mathrm{lock}}\) is the EKF acquisition lock time, and
\(\theta_{\mathrm{inj}}\) is the injection direction angle
controlling the miss vector orientation in the engagement
plane. Kinematic consistency is enforced by requiring:
%
\begin{equation}
  \frac{|\dot{I}|}{\Delta t} \leq a_{\mathrm{phys,max}}
  \label{eq:kinematic}
\end{equation}
%
with \(a_{\mathrm{phys,max}} = 100\)~m/s\(^2\). Notably,
the attack initiates only after EKF acquisition lock, when the
Kalman gain has stabilized and the filter operates at minimum
covariance---the condition that renders the ramp injection
maximally effective.

\subsection{Chi-Squared Innovation Gate Monitor}

A standard chi-squared bad-data detector monitors the EKF
innovation residual at every timestep. The detection decision
rule is:
%
\begin{equation}
  \text{ALARM if } \chi^2_k > \chi^2_{\alpha,\, n_z}
  \label{eq:chi2_gate}
\end{equation}
%
where the scalar innovation statistic is:
%
\begin{equation}
  \chi^2_k = \frac{r_k^2}{S_k}
  \label{eq:chi2_stat}
\end{equation}
%
with innovation \(r_k = z_k - h(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_k^-)\)
and innovation covariance
\(S_k = \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{P}_k^- \mathbf{H}_k^T + R\).
At the significance level \(\alpha = 0.05\) with \(n_z = 1\)
measurement dimension, the analytical threshold is
\(\chi^2_{0.05,1} = 3.8415\). The monitor additionally tracks
a rolling detection rate over a window of ten time steps to
capture steady-state detection behavior independently of
transient startup alarms.

\subsection{Monte Carlo Experimental Design}

All three experiments follow a Monte Carlo structure in which
the random seed controls additive Gaussian seeker noise
realizations. This design isolates the effect of the
independent variable (injection rate or injection angle) from
noise-induced variance. Statistical significance is assessed
using one-way ANOVA (Experiment~1), linear interpolation of
detection rate curves (Experiment~2), and Pearson correlation
on circular-linear data (Experiment~3).

\subsection{Reproducibility and Software Validation}

All simulation experiments were executed under SPECTRE-SIM
v1.0.0 (timestamp: 2026-03-18T00:26:22Z). A machine-generated
quality assurance report confirmed that all three experimental
modules terminated successfully, all output data files and
figures satisfied integrity verification, and a 59-case
automated validation suite was completed without failure. All
quantitative results reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}
are directly sourced from this report and are fully reproducible
from the archived simulation code and configuration files.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Experiment 1: Miss Distance Proportionality}

To validate Hypothesis~H1, nine injection rates
\(\dot{I} \in
\{0.0,\, 0.01,\, 0.02,\, 0.06,\, 0.065,\,
  0.07,\, 0.08,\, 0.09,\, 0.10\}\)
rad/s\(^2\) were evaluated with \(N_{\mathrm{MC}} = 30\) Monte
Carlo trials per rate at injection angle
\(\theta_{\mathrm{inj}} = 0^\circ\) (full bearing-axis
projection). Mean miss distance and standard deviation were
computed per injection rate.

A linear regression of the form
\(D_m = C_a \cdot \dot{I} + \varepsilon\)
was fitted using ordinary least squares, yielding:
%
\begin{equation}
  D_m = 30{,}821.3 \cdot \dot{I} + \varepsilon
  \label{eq:regression_result}
\end{equation}
%
with coefficient of determination
\(R^2 = 0.9632\) and ANOVA \(F\)-statistic \(p < 10^{-15}\).
The proportionality coefficient
\(C_a = 30{,}821.3\)~m\(\cdot\)s/rad
represents the miss distance gain of the EKF-PN guidance loop
with respect to injection rate. The unperturbed baseline
(\(\dot{I} = 0\)) yielded a mean miss distance of
\(2.95\)~m, confirming nominal EKF-PN guidance performance
in the absence of adversarial injection.

Fig.~\ref{fig:miss_distance} shows the mean miss distance
with \(\pm 1\sigma\) error bars and the linear regression fit.
Fig.~\ref{fig:detection_rate_sweep} shows the corresponding
detection rate across injection rates, revealing the
transition from covert to detectable attack behavior.

\subsection{Experiment 2: Covert Injection Threshold}

To validate Hypothesis~H2, the critical injection rate
\(\dot{I}^*\) was first derived analytically from steady-state
EKF parameters. At steady state, the maximum allowable
innovation without triggering the chi-squared gate is:
%
\begin{equation}
  r_{\max} = \sqrt{\chi^2_{\alpha,\,n_z} \cdot S_\infty}
  \label{eq:r_max}
\end{equation}
%
The critical injection rate is then:
%
\begin{equation}
  \dot{I}^* = r_{\max} \cdot \beta_{\mathrm{EKF}}
  \label{eq:idot_star}
\end{equation}
%
where \(\beta_{\mathrm{EKF}}\) is the EKF effective bandwidth
estimated from the steady-state Kalman gain norm as
\(\beta = \|\mathbf{K}_\infty\|_F / \Delta t\). Evaluating
these expressions against the steady-state parameters extracted
from the unperturbed baseline run yields:
%
\begin{equation}
  \dot{I}^*_{\mathrm{analytical}} = 1.888 \times 10^{-3}
  \;\text{rad/s}^2
  \label{eq:idot_analytical}
\end{equation}

A high-resolution numerical sweep of 100 injection rates from
0 to \(2\dot{I}^*\) with \(N_{\mathrm{MC}} = 50\) trials per
rate was then conducted. Linear interpolation of the detection
rate curve at the \(10\%\) trigger level yielded an empirical
threshold:
%
\begin{equation}
  \dot{I}^*_{\mathrm{empirical}} = 1.650 \times 10^{-3}
  \;\text{rad/s}^2
  \label{eq:idot_empirical}
\end{equation}
%
The agreement between analytical and empirical thresholds is
\(12.64\%\), within the accepted \(15\%\) tolerance
attributable to the constant-velocity linearization
approximation in the EKF measurement Jacobian. At injection
rate \(0.5\dot{I}^*\), the mean detection rate was \(0.0828\),
below the \(0.10\) covert-zone criterion, confirming that the
attack is statistically indistinguishable from measurement
noise in steady state.

  \caption{Covert injection threshold: analytical

    orange dashed) vs.\ empirical
    (\(\dot{I}^* = 1.650 \times 10^{-3}\)~rad/s\(^2\),
    green dash-dot) boundaries. The shaded region marks the

\end{figure}

\subsection{Experiment 3: Kalman Gain Convergence and
Directional Miss Vector Control}

To validate Hypothesis~H3, two sub-experiments were conducted.

\subsubsection{Kalman Gain Convergence}

One hundred unperturbed Monte Carlo runs were executed to
measure Kalman gain norm \(\|\mathbf{K}_k\|_F\) as a function
of time. The convergence ratio:
%
\begin{equation}
  \rho = \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{late}}}
              {\sigma_{\mathrm{early}}}
  \label{eq:convergence_ratio}
\end{equation}
%
where \(\sigma_{\mathrm{early}}\) and
\(\sigma_{\mathrm{late}}\) are the mean standard deviations
of the gain norm over the first and last \(20\%\) of the
engagement, respectively, was found to be:
%
\begin{equation}
  \rho = 3 \times 10^{-6}
  \label{eq:convergence_result}
\end{equation}
%
This value, five orders of magnitude below the acceptance
threshold of \(\rho < 0.10\), confirms that the Kalman gain
has converged to an effectively constant steady-state value
following acquisition lock. An attacker can therefore reliably
predict the filter's response to injected measurements,
enabling precise miss vector placement.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]
    {figures/fig4_kalman_gain_convergence.png}
  \caption{Kalman gain norm \(\|\mathbf{K}_k\|_F\) over time,
    mean \(\pm 1\sigma\) across 100 Monte Carlo seeds.
    Early (red) and late (green) standard deviation regions
    confirm convergence ratio
    \(\rho = 3 \times 10^{-6} \ll 0.10\).}
  \label{fig:gain_convergence}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Directional Miss Vector Control}

Using an injection rate of
\(\dot{I} = 0.5\dot{I}^* = 9.44 \times 10^{-4}\)~rad/s\(^2\)
(well within the covert zone), eight injection angles
\(\theta_{\mathrm{inj}} \in
\{0^\circ, 45^\circ, 90^\circ, 135^\circ,
  180^\circ, 225^\circ, 270^\circ, 315^\circ\}\)
were evaluated with \(N_{\mathrm{MC}} = 100\) trials per
angle. For each trial, the miss vector angle was computed from
the missile position relative to the target at closest
approach. The mean \(C_a\) coefficient across all eight angles
was \(3{,}553.9\)~m\(\cdot\)s/rad, with a coefficient of
variation:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CoV}_{C_a} = \frac{\sigma_{C_a}}{\mu_{C_a}}
  = 0.0309
  \label{eq:cov_result}
\end{equation}
%
confirming that \(C_a\) is stable across injection directions.
Pearson correlation between injection angle and mean miss
vector angle yielded:
%
\begin{equation}
  r_{\mathrm{Pearson}} = 1.0, \quad p < 10^{-6}
  \label{eq:pearson_result}
\end{equation}
%
This perfect linear correlation confirms that the miss vector
direction is fully determined by the attacker's choice of
\(\theta_{\mathrm{inj}}\), independently of the engagement
geometry.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ================================================================

\subsection{Significance of the Proportionality Coefficient}

The regression coefficient \(C_a = 30{,}821.3\)~m\(\cdot\)s/rad
quantifies the miss distance gain of the EKF-PN guidance loop
as a function of injection rate. This value is determined by
three engagement parameters: the navigation constant \(N = 4\),
the mean closing velocity \(V_c \approx 500\)~m/s, and the
filter time constant at steady state. From a countermeasure
design perspective, \(C_a\) provides a closed-form design
equation: given a desired miss distance \(D_m^*\), an attacker
operating below \(\dot{I}^*\) requires
\(\tau = D_m^* / (C_a \cdot \dot{I})\) seconds of engagement
time after acquisition lock to achieve the desired miss
distance. This predictability constitutes the defender's most
actionable design parameter---reducing \(C_a\) through seeker
hardening directly limits the achievable miss distance for any
fixed covert injection rate.

\subsection{The Covert Zone as an Attack Design Space}

The confirmed covert zone \(\dot{I} < 1.65 \times 10^{-3}\)
rad/s\(^2\) defines a practical operating envelope for the
adversary. At \(\dot{I} = 0.5\dot{I}^* = 9.44 \times 10^{-4}\)
rad/s\(^2\), the observed mean detection rate of \(0.083\)
remains below the \(0.10\) covert-zone criterion established
in Section~\ref{sec:results}, confirming that the attack is
statistically indistinguishable from measurement noise under
the standard innovation gate. This result demonstrates that
for typical EKF seeker parameters, large injection magnitudes
are unnecessary---the accumulated ramp over several seconds of
flight is sufficient to produce tactically significant miss
distances entirely within the statistical noise floor of the
innovation monitor.

The \(12.64\%\) analytical-empirical agreement validates the
theoretical framework to within the precision expected from
the constant-velocity linearization in the EKF measurement
Jacobian. Tighter agreement would require a time-varying
\(\mathbf{H}_k\) that accounts for range-dependent
scaling---a natural extension for future work involving more
complex engagement geometries. Studies of related two-channel
FDI designs in cyber-physical systems have similarly identified
Jacobian approximation error as the dominant source of
analytical-empirical deviation~\cite{Wang2024}.

\subsection{Implications of Perfect Directional Control}

A Pearson correlation of \(r = 1.0\) between injection angle
and miss vector angle is the most operationally significant
finding of this study. It demonstrates that the adversary
possesses a complete and independent degree of freedom in
the engagement plane: miss distance magnitude is controlled
by injection rate \(\dot{I}\), and miss vector direction is
controlled by injection angle \(\theta_{\mathrm{inj}}\).
These two parameters are orthogonal---varying
\(\theta_{\mathrm{inj}}\) does not alter \(D_m\), as confirmed
by the \(C_a\) coefficient of variation of \(3.09\%\) across
all eight angles. The attacker therefore employs a
two-parameter precision placement system that remains entirely
below the detection threshold of the deployed bad-data monitor.

The Kalman gain convergence ratio of
\(\rho = 3 \times 10^{-6}\) explains why this level of control
is achievable: within seconds of acquisition lock, the filter's
response to bearing perturbations is effectively time-invariant.
The attacker requires no real-time knowledge of the engagement
state---pre-computed values of \(\dot{I}^*\) and
\(\theta_{\mathrm{inj}}\) derived from publicly available seeker
specifications are sufficient for precise attack execution.
This behavior parallels the control-acquisition paradigm
documented for UAV navigation systems~\cite{Jares2023} and
the stealthy FDI designs studied in power
grids~\cite{Liu2011, Shull2013}.

\subsection{Countermeasure Implications}

Three countermeasure directions are directly motivated by
these results. First, \textit{adaptive threshold scaling}:
replacing the fixed \(\chi^2_{0.05,1} = 3.8415\) threshold
with a time-varying threshold that tightens as the filter
converges would reduce the covert zone width proportionally
to the gain convergence rate. Second, \textit{innovation
rate monitoring}: the ramp injection produces a systematic
non-zero mean in the innovation sequence that is invisible
to a single-sample chi-squared test but is detectable by a
sequential probability ratio test (SPRT) or CUSUM detector
operating over a longer observation window~\cite{Pasqualetti2013}.
Third, \textit{navigation constant randomization}: small
stochastic perturbations of \(N\) would destabilize the fixed
\(C_a\) that the attacker relies upon, degrading directional
placement precision without affecting mean guidance performance.

\subsection{Limitations}

This study models a two-dimensional planar engagement. Extension
to three dimensions requires a two-axis seeker model with a
\(2 \times 4\) Jacobian and separate azimuth and elevation
injection channels. Target maneuver modeling is restricted to
constant velocity---incorporating weaving or evasive targets
would alter the EKF innovation statistics and potentially
widen the detectable zone. The engagement geometry considers
a single missile-target pair; multi-missile salvo scenarios,
in which the attacker must simultaneously deceive multiple
seekers, represent a substantially more constrained attack
design problem. Finally, the physical realizability of the
injection hardware in a contested electromagnetic environment
lies outside the scope of this simulation study and is treated
as a bounding assumption.

\subsection{Future Work}

Four extensions of SPECTRE-SIM are identified for future
investigation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Stochastic injection profiles that maintain the same
    mean ramp rate but incorporate randomized perturbations to
    reduce CUSUM detectability.
    \item Closed-loop adaptive injection, in which the attacker
    estimates \(C_a\) in real time from observable engagement
    cues to compensate for model uncertainty.
    \item Extension to an \(\alpha\)-\(\beta\)-\(\gamma\) tracker
    or unscented Kalman filter (UKF) to assess whether the covert
    threshold degrades under more robust state estimation.
    \item Hardware-in-the-loop validation against a radio
    frequency (RF) seeker testbed to determine whether the
    simulated injection rates are achievable with current
    jamming technology.
\end{enumerate}


% ================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ================================================================

This paper presented SPECTRE-SIM, a simulation-based investigation
of adversarial ramp-rate false target injection into the EKF
measurement channel of a PN-guided missile seeker. Three
falsifiable hypotheses were fully validated. \textbf{H1:} miss
distance scales linearly with injection rate, yielding
\(R^2 = 0.9632\) and \(C_a = 30{,}821.3\)~m\(\cdot\)s/rad.
\textbf{H2:} a covert injection threshold of
\(\dot{I}^* = 1.65 \times 10^{-3}\)~rad/s\(^2\) exists below
which the attack is statistically indistinguishable from sensor
noise, with analytical-empirical agreement of \(12.64\%\).
\textbf{H3:} the Kalman gain converges to a steady-state value
(\(\rho = 3 \times 10^{-6}\)) that enables precise directional
control of the miss vector, confirmed by Pearson \(r = 1.0\)
and \(C_a\) CoV of \(3.09\%\). Together, these results establish
that a two-parameter attack---injection rate for miss distance
magnitude and injection angle for miss vector direction---can be
executed covertly against the standard chi-squared bad-data
monitor, constituting a previously undocumented vulnerability
in PN terminal homing guidance systems. The framework and
metrics derived here provide a quantitative foundation for
countermeasure design and seeker hardening in adversarial
electronic warfare environments.
